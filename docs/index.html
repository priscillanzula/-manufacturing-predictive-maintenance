Key findings
01
Life stage is the dominant signal 94.5% importance
cycle_ratio alone drives 94.5% of the model’s predictive power. No single raw sensor comes close. Where an engine is in its lifecycle matters far more than instantaneous readings.
02
7 sensors carry zero signal drop
sensor_1, 5, 6, 10, 16, 18, 19 are constant across all 20,631 readings. Removing them cut model complexity with zero accuracy loss.
03
Pressure & temperature degrade predictably r = 0.70
sensor_11 (HPC static pressure, r = 0.696) and sensor_4 (LPT temperature, r = 0.679) show the strongest monotonic correlation with remaining life.
04
Wide lifespan variance across fleet 128–362 cycles
Engines vary enormously — shortest life 128 cycles, longest 362. Standard deviation of 46 cycles. This variance is exactly why predictive beats scheduled maintenance.
05
Model provides 3–4 week advance warning
With RMSE of 7.2 cycles and the danger threshold at 30 cycles, maintenance teams get a reliable window of ~23 cycles to plan and execute intervention before failure.
Recommendations
Ground 25 engines immediately
Engines with RUL < 30 cycles must be pulled from service. At $500k+ per unplanned failure, preventing a single incident recoups the entire cost of this system. Prioritise engines #34, #31, #81, #68 — all at RUL ≤ 8.
Schedule 20 warning engines within one maintenance cycle
Monitor sensor_11 rolling average and sensor_7 rolling average — these are the earliest-moving degradation signals. Trigger alerts when either crosses 1.5 std dev from baseline.
Automate daily alerts via GitHub Actions
send_alerts.py is production-ready. Schedule it as a daily cron job. This ensures no engine crosses the danger threshold between manual checks.
Extend model to FD002–FD004 for real-world generalisation
Current model is trained on one operating condition. FD002–FD004 add five more. Re-training with op_setting features would make the system fleet-ready for mixed operations.
